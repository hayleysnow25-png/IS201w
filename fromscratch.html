The Ancient Art of Hawaiian Outrigger Canoeing

Discover the Tradition

A Living Connection to the Sea

Long before GPS or modern vessels, Polynesian navigators crossed thousands of miles of open Pacific Ocean in outrigger canoes — guided only by stars, ocean swells, and the direction of wind. In Hawaiʻi, the outrigger canoe (waʻa) is not merely a sport: it is a vessel of cultural identity, community, and deep spiritual connection to the ʻāina (land) and kai (sea).

Today, tens of thousands of paddlers worldwide compete in sprint races, long-distance channel crossings, and surf zone events. But every stroke traces back to the same ancient tradition. The Anatomy of a Waʻa

Understanding the canoe and its crew is the first step in appreciating the sport.

  1. The Canoe (Waʻa)
    • Ama — The outrigger float on the left side; provides lateral stability in rough water
    • ʻIako — Two curved wooden spars that connect the ama to the main hull
    • Manu Ihu — The carved bow piece at the front, designed to cut through waves
    • Manu Hope — The carved stern piece at the rear of the hull
    • Pola — The deck area between the ʻiako where balance is maintained
  2. The Crew Positions (6-Person OC6)
    • Seat 1 — The Stroker: Sets the pace and cadence for the entire boat; must be a powerful and consistent paddler
    • Seat 2 — The Caller: Calls the side-switch command ("hut"), supports the stroker's rhythm
    • Seats 3 & 4 — The Engine Room: Pure power positions; these paddlers provide the bulk of the boat's forward drive
    • Seat 5 — Support Power: Adds power and helps stabilize the canoe through rough water
    • Seat 6 — The Steersman: Steers with a longer paddle, reads the ocean, and makes tactical decisions
  3. The Major Races
    • Molokaī Hoe — 41-mile men's channel race from Moloka'i to Oahu; held every October
    • Nā Wāhine O Ke Kai — 41-mile women's channel race across the Kaiwi Channel
    • Queen Liliʻuokalani Race — World's largest outrigger canoe race, held in Kailua-Kona each Labor Day weekend
    • OC1 World Championships — Solo outrigger canoe world championship events
    • Sprint Regattas — Short-distance sprint races held throughout the Hawaiian islands year-round
